** Star Formation: Theories

*** Time Scales

$$t_{{\rm ff}}=\sqrt{\frac{3\pi}{32G\rho_{0}}} \approx 43.5 \, {\rm Myr} \cdot n^{-1/2},$$
assuming a mean molecular weight $\mu = 1.4$.

$$c_{s}=\sqrt{\gamma \frac{p_{0}}{\rho_{0}}} = \sqrt{\gamma \frac{kT}{\mu m_{H}}}
= 0.313~{\rm km/s} \cdot T_{1}^{\;1/2}.
% = 9.91~{\rm km/s} \cdot T_{4}^{\;1/2}.$$

\begin{align*}
  v_{\rm vir,orb} = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{R}} &= 6.6~{\rm km~s^{-1}} \; M_4^{1/2} R_{\rm pc}^{-1/2} \\
  &= 30~{\rm km~s^{-1}} \; M^{1/2} R_{\rm AU}^{-1/2}
\end{align*}

*** Cooling

Radiative cooling at $T>10^6$ K is dominated by the bremsstrahlung
volume cooling rate:
$${\cal E}_{\rm ff} = \int \varepsilon_{\rm ff}(\nu) \ d \nu 
  \approx 1.4\times 10^{-23} 1.4\e{-23} T_8^{1/2} \left( \frac{n_e}{{\rm cm}^{-3}} \right)^2
  {\rm erg} {\rm s}^{-1} {\rm cm}^{-3}$$ assuming a charge number of unity ($Z=1$)
and $n_i \sim n_e$.

*** Dynamics

Dynamical friction: 

\begin{align*}
  \frac{\dif \mathbf{v}_M}{\dif t} &= D[\Delta v] \approx \frac{4 \sqrt{2\pi}}{3} G^2 M \rho
  \ln \Lambda \frac{ {\bf v}_M}{\sigma^3} \;\;\; (v_M \; \mathrm{small}),
  \\
  \frac{\dif {\bf v}_M}{\dif t} &= D[\Delta v] \approx 4\pi G^2 M \rho
  \ln \Lambda \frac{{\bf v}_M}{v_M^3} \;\;\; (v_M \; \mathrm{large}),
\end{align*}

where
$$\Lambda = \frac{b_{\rm max}}{b_{90}} \approx \frac{\cal M}{M} \frac{R_{\rm
  orb}}{\cal R}.$$

*** Radiative Recombination of Hydrogen
    :PROPERTIES:
    :CUSTOM_ID: sec:recomb
    :END:

Case B recombination: optically thick to radiation just above $I_{\rm
H}=13.60$ eV, so that ionizing photons emitted during recombination
are immediately reabsorbed, creating another ion and free electron by
photoionization.

Following Draine Ch 15.1.1, let $Q_{0}$ be the rate of emission of
hydrogen-ionizing photons, i.e., $h\nu>I_{\rm H}=13.6$ eV. Equating
the rates of photoionization and radiative recombination gives the
ionization balance equation:

$$Q_{0}=\frac{4\pi}{3}R_{{\rm S}}^{\;3}\,\alpha\,n(H^{+})\,n_{e}.$$

Since $n(H^{+})=n_{e}=n_{H}\equiv n_{0}$, we can solve for the
/Stromgren radius/ Draine2011 [Eq. 15.1 (pg 163)]

$$R_{S}\equiv\left(\frac{3Q_{0}}{4\pi\,n_{0}^{\;2}\,\alpha_{B}}\right)^{1/3}
= (9.77\e{18} {\rm cm}, {\rm or} \; 3.17 ~{\rm pc}) \, Q_{49}^{1/3} \,
n_2^{-2/3} \, T_4^{0.28},$$

with 

$$\alpha(T_e) \approx 2 \times 10^{-13} \left( \frac{T_e}{10^4 {\rm
K}} \right)^{-3/4} \, {\rm cm}^3 \, {\rm s}^{-1}.$$

Draine also talked about other aspects of region, including the mean
emission measure, mean free path of an 18 eV photon at
$T<50,000$ K, and helium ionization.

*** Ionization Time Scales
    :PROPERTIES:
    :CUSTOM_ID: ionization-time-scales
    :END:

How long will it take to ionize an region?

$$\tau_{{\rm ioni.}}=\frac{4/3\,\pi R_{{\rm S}}^{\;3}\,n_{0}}{Q_{0}} =
\frac{1}{\alpha_{B}\,n_{0}}=\frac{1.22\times10^{3}\,{\rm
yr}}{n_{2}}.$$

for $T \sim 10^4$ K. The /recombination time scale/ (after the
ionizing source turns off) is identical to the ionization time
scale. For $n_{0}>0.03$ cm$^{-3}$, this time scale is shorter than the
main-sequence lifetime $\gtrsim$ 5 Myr for a massive star.

*** Metallicity
    :PROPERTIES:
    :CUSTOM_ID: metallicity
    :END:

Solar metallicity is $Z = 0.0134$ \cite{Asplund2009}, where $Z$ is the
heavy element mass fraction. The *metallicity* relative to solar is
$$[{\rm Fe}/{\rm H}] \equiv \log_{10} \left[ \frac{(N_{\rm Fe}/N_{\rm
H})_{\rm star}}{(N_{\rm Fe}/N_H)_{\odot}} \right],$$ Stars with [Fe/H]
< 0 are metal-poor relative to the Sun. Metal poor makes stars bluer
and metal rich makes stars redder due to line blanketing (metals
absorb preferentially blue light) and opacity effect (metals absorb
energy from the interior of the star, making red giants "swell up"
even more, and give them cooler temperatures.)

**** Sputtering in Hot Gas
     :PROPERTIES:
     :CUSTOM_ID: sputtering-in-hot-gas
     :END:

[Draine Sec. 25.7] Grain lifetime of a stationary grain is given by

$$\tau = 10^5 (1 + T_6^{-3}) \frac{a/0.1 \mu{\rm m}}{n_{\rm H}/{\rm
cm}^{-3}} \; {\rm yr}.$$

In HII region with $T = 10^4~{\rm K}$ and
$n_{\rm H} = 10^4~{\rm cm^{-3}}$, $\tau = 10$ Myr for grains with size
0.1 micron. With $n_{\rm H} = 10^2~{\rm cm^{-3}}$, $\tau = 1000$ Myr, and
if the temperature increases to $10^5$ K, $\tau = 1$ Myr.
